Brainster on the go

A gamified mobile e-learning application, that helps users find their right career path and focus on developing the right skills without a hustle.

Jump to results

The challenge

In the competitive world, everyone wants to be skilled. The first challenge for younger people, and people looking to change their careers is to find their next career path and later on pursue it, and develop and improve their skillsets, making them competitive on the market.

The solution

Brainster on the go is an application that allows users to find their career paths and develop skills without a hustle using gamification mechanics.

My role

Conducting stakeholder and user interview, User research, Ideate, User journey map, Wireframes, Prototyping, Iterating on designs and Usability testing to evaluate the new designs.

Research

I conducted extensive research on theoretical findings about the educational psychology of learning. In this field, one author Zimmerman points out with his model of self-regulated learning.

Self-learning model

Forethought Phase

This is also known as a planning phase, where learners set achievable short- or long-term goals, choose strategies that best address the pursue of those goals, and assess their beliefs and interest in the learning environment.

Performance Phase

Learners implement strategies to make ongoing adjustments to progress toward their goals via self-monitoring and self-control.

Self-reflection Phase

Learners evaluate the effectiveness of their strategies and examine their satisfaction with the learning tasks. Feedback from this self-reflection phase will then apply to the start of the next SRL cycle.

Self-regulated learning is a cyclical process, wherein the student plans for a task, monitors their performance, and then reflects on the outcome. The cycle then repeats as the student uses the reflection to adjust and prepare for the next task.

I discovered another two university researchers that dealt with applying Zimmermans self-learning model in online learning platforms, Rebecca Cheng, Ph.D. and Iris Poon. They applied the self-learning model on two different e-learning platforms: Duolinguo and Codeacademy. By analyzing both findings I managed to get insight and define the must haves in my application:

Self-learning model

Forethought Phase
  • Daily goals
  • Progress bar
  • Social persuasion
  • Brif tutorials (Intristic motivation)
Performance Phase
  • Experience points
  • Achievement board
  • Awards
  • Badges of recognition
  • Gamification
Self-reflection Phase
  • Weekly tests
  • Achievement board
  • Networking

Challenging the theoretical findings

To challenge the theoretical findings, I made a questionnaire covering the applications functionalities that based on the Zimmermans model are necessary for success. In demographic aspect, I targeted the primary target audience:

  • High School students (Senior preferred), but not limited to.
  • 23-28 yo’s looking to change their careers (trial & error before taking a path)

30 high-school students answered the questionary. I summarized the following findings (hypothesis) :

1.

Our user doesn’t know what UX is

2.

Majority users find it pointless if they can’t measure their progress

3.

Online learning is a familiar concept (because of Covid)

4.

Users want to connect with other users and mentors

5.

Our user doesn’t have the motivation nor determination to go for a long-hour courses

6.

Our users aren’t confident about their capabilities for pursuing UX/UI career

7.

Our users have 100% openess for using a learning app

8.

Our user needs a daily reminders for using the app, lack of self-discipline is acknowled

Creating habits

The learning process follows a 7-day learning model. Each day the user gets a different type of lesson, collecting experience points in the process that helps them rank higher on the global leaderboard.

Once selected, the user has an option to start the quiz or watch a lesson. Watching a lesson isn’t obligatory, but gives additional points and for gamification purposes it stimulates loyal user to watch short video tutorials.

As mentioned before, every day has a different modality for knowledge transfer. This example shows answers chosen from 4 different pictures and the outcomes. Then I introduced the popular tinder swipe (left or right) which is very familiar to our target users and good for the learnability of the overall app.

Encouraging the users

With the success stories blog posting I target the indecisive and self-doubt users. With the questionnaire, I found that our user persona has trouble with initially starting and choosing a future career path or change. In this way, I want to encourage them by showing good examples of ordinary people from their demographic group.

Gamification tools for encouraging the user to proceed with learning and develop a routine for app usage.

The Profile part, covers all the achievements which stay in line with Zimmermans model about self-reflection and students reevaluating the progress. The friends option connects the app user with other users, helps in networking, developing competitive environment and speeds the up the application usage among users. Push notifications are introduced as a daily reminder to the user.

Usability testing

Once completed with a fully clickable high-fidelity prototype I went for a user testing scenario to find out eventual flaws in the design or the flow.

I used the Loop11 online tool for user testing. The thinking-aloud method was determined to stimulate the user to express loudly his opinions while interacting with the interface. I shortly explained the purpose of the research to my user tester. I gave him a scenario starting point and I stressed that talking loudly and explaining the reasoning behind every click is crucial and from that moment I stopped talking. Here I defined the pain points of my current design which need to be addressed in the future:

1.

Onboarding: I will definitely try the free version first, but what knowledge do I get for free

2.

Registration - why I just can’t connect with my google account (only one click)

3.

I prefer the swipe answers

4.

Home page- why I can’t decide how much time to spend on the app (like in Duolingo) - Jacobs law

5.

Networking- What is the thing with the QR code

Once finished with the testing, the user-tester was given a 10-question System Usability Scale (SUS) to answer. The result was 82 points with an acceptability range of 80. The reason I decided to go with the SUS was to determine whether I am following the user-centered design principles and going in line with the user needs.

Summary

Sustainable e-learning systems are dependent on competent self-regulated learners. On the other hand, applying Zimmerman’s self-regulated learning model in an e-learning system can teach self-regulation and guide students’ work by instilling the process of planning, practicing, evaluating, and adjusting. This in turn allows students to assume full control and accountability for their learning. For the purpose of this project, I implemented the well accepted functionalities and gamification methods that Duolinguo uses, in order to reduce the application learnability time, reduce the users cognitive load and create an intuitive and user-friendly design. Once on the website, the user won’t be bothered with learning how to use the app, his only focus will be the actual learning which is our primary objective.
